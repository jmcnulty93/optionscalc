Black Scholes:

Suppose XYX stock is trading 51.00.
XYZ does not pay dividends. The current risk free rate is 0.00%.
Consider a Call Option on XYZ that has a Strike Price of 55.
The Call expires in 30 days and cannot be exercised before exipiration. (European Style)

At expiration the Call Option will be worth XYZ - Strike if the XYZ > Strike, otherwise it
will have a mimimum value of zero. Therefore, the Call Payout at Expiration is zero or XYZ-Strike.
There will only be a positive payout if XYZ is above Strike at Expiration.
When the Stock Price is above the Strike Price, the Strike or Option is considered to be In-The-Money.

The Black Scholes Model tries to answer the question: What is the Call Option worth today?
(for a European Style option)

To do this model assigns a probabilty to the Strike Price being In-The-Money at Expiration.
In other words, the probability that XYZ is greater than the Strike at Expiration.
In the model, N(d2) represents the probabilty that the Stock Price is greater than the Strike Price
at Expiration.
If the Stock Px is greater than the Strike Px at Expiration (the Strike is In-The-Money), then the Call
will be exersised and the owner of the Call will pay the Strike Px for the Stock. This will happen in the
future, at expiration. Therefore, we will be concerned with the probability of paying the Strike Px in the
future. In today's terms that is the probabilty of paying Present Value of the Strike.

If XYZ is greater than the Strike at Expiration, then the Call will have a positive payout.
So, Black Sholes also must consider all possible positve payouts and assign them a probabilty
to generate an average probability weighted payout.


Black Scholes needs 5 inputs:
1. S = Current Stock Px (less that Present Value of Dividends that go Ex before Expiration)
2. K = Strike Price
3. t = Time to Expiration
4. r = Risk Free Rate
5. vol = Volatility (future Volatility: now to expiration)


All inputs are known except for the Volatility (Vol).
Vol is an annualized standard deviation.
We need the future Vol. A typical assumption to make is that future Vol will be similar to past Vol.
The option we are considering has 30 days to Expiration. So, we might look at the past 30 days of stock
price data. We can calculate the standard deviation of the past 30 calendar days of closing prices.
On a calendar, some of those days will be weekends or holidays. So, we will have less than 30 closing prices.
However, there are still 30 days to Expiration. So, for the Time to Expiration input we will use 30/365.
A calendar month will have roughly 21 trading days. A calendar year will have roughly 252 trading days.
So, when we calc the std dev of the daily closing prices of the stock we will annualize it be multiplying it
by the square root of 252. If we calc'd the std dev of weekly closes, then we would annualize by multiplying
it by the square root of 365/7 (the # of weeks per yr). If it were monthly closes then the sqrt of 12.
It could be a shorter time period. Perhaps the stock price in 10 minute intervals.
The key is to think: How many data points would I collect in a year. Then multiply by the sqrt of that number.

Our option has 30 days to expiration and we are trying to estimate a future vol for this time period
based on the past 30 calendar days of daily closing prices in the stock (approx 21). This is a rather simple scheme.
It could be more complicated to capture more data. We could use a rolling 30-day average for the past year.
We could weight each 30 day std dev (which has been annualized) to have a weighted rolling 30-day average.
Essentially, you could use any scheme you think is logical to model the stock's future volatiltiy for the
time period you need.

Once you have an estimate of future volatility, it can be used to create a probability distribution and therefore
a probability.

Vol * sqrt(t) = One Std Dev for the time peridod.
2 * (Vol * sqrt(t)) = Two Std Dev or the time period.
These can be translated into stock prices too:
StockPx * e^(Vol * sqrt(t)) = One Std Dev Up
StockPx * e^(2*(Vol * sqrt(t))) = Two Std Dev Up
StockPx * e^(-Vol * sqrt(t)) = One Std Dev Down
StockPx * e^(-2*(Vol * sqrt(t))) = Two Std Dev Down

A major assumption of the Black Sholes Model is that the percentage returns for a given period (daily, weekly, monthly, 5 minute)
are Normally Distributed. A Normal Distrubtion can give you a proabilty of an item being greater or less than a certain threshold
if you can define how std devs the item is from the mean of the distribution. In the BS Model we calculate how many std devs the
Strike Price (K) is from from the Current Stock Price (S):

Number of Std Dev K from S = LN(K/S) / (vol * SQRT(time))

LN(K/S) is the natural log of K/S.
K/S tells us how far K is from S in simple percent. However, we always need the Continously Compunded rate of return.
In the example above: S = 51 and K = 55
K/S = 55/51 = 1.07843 ....K is 7.843% greater than S
LN(K/S) = .07551 ...this is: what exponent of e makes e^x = K/S...the continous rate of return in which K is greater than S.




The BS model makes use of the statistics of a Normal Distribution.
A set of data will have a mean. Each item of data will fall a certain number of standard deviations from the mean.
The data set is also referred to as a distribution. The mean of the data set is also the mean of the distribution.
The closing prices of the stock ticker AAPL for the past 90 days is an exaple of a data set or distribution.
As a general rule of thumb: a data set follows a Normal Distribution if roughly 68.2% of the items fall within 1 stdev
from the mean, 95.4% of the items fall within 2 stdev and 99.6% of the items fall within 3 stdev.
The amount of standard devaitions an item in the data set falls from the mean is known as the item's Z-Score.
Also 50% of the items will fall above the mean and 50% will fall below.
You are probably familar with the Bell Curve:

This Bell Curve or Normal Distribution depicted here has a mean of zero and a standard deviaton of 1.
A Normal Distribution with a mean of zero and stdev of 1 is known as the Standard Normal Distribution.
On the graph of the Standard Normal Distribution above, you see standard deviations plotted on the x-axis.
In other words the x values are the standard devations you see.
You can obtain the y values on the graph by feeding each x value into the equation below:
(this is a graph of the Standard Normal curve, so you need to use a mean of zero and a standard deviation of 1)
With an x value and y value you can plot the Standard Normal Curve.
A Standard Normal Distribution is a data set that has a mean of zero and a standard deviation of 1 and follows the general
rule of thumb (68-95-99).

With this curve, a probabiltiy distribution can be constructed for our data set.
To consider probability we must focus on the area between the x-axis and the bell curve.
This area us usually referred to the area under the curve.

Consider the std dev or Z-Score of 2.00.
2.00 is the x-value we will feed into the equation for the bell curve using a mean of zero and a std dev of 1 in the equation.
This will produce a y-value of 0.053991.
Now we have an (x,y) coordinate of (2.00, 0.053991). Take a look at the Standard Normal Bell Curve.
You can visually estimate that this point falls right on the curve.

We could have chosen any Z-Score (x-value) for the curve equation to produce a y-value and therefore
a coordinate that falls on the curve. We could have chosen -0.55 or -0.550001...there are an infinite number
of Z-Scores we can feed through the equation. Therefore there an infinte number of y-values and an infinite number of
points on the curve.

Let's take a look at the Z-Score of 1.00 and the Z-Score of 0.00. Find this on the x-axis of the Normal Standard Curve graph.
Remember the Standard Normal Distribution (which is represented in the graph) has a mean of zero and a standard deviation of 1.
In the Standard Normal Distribution, 68.2% of the items in the data set will have a Z-Score (standard deviation) within
one standard deviation of the mean, which is zero. Another way to state this is that 68.2% of the items in the distribution will
have a Z-Score between -1.00 and 1.00.
Don't confuse the Z-Score of an item in the distribution with the standard deviation of the distribution.
The x-axis of the graph is showing Z-Scores (standard deviation from the mean).
If the distribution is Standard Normal then 68.2% of the items will have a Z-Score between -1.00 and 1.00
because the distribution has a mean of zero and a standard deviation of 1.
Likewise, as you can see on the graph, 95.4% of the items in the distribution have a Z-Score -2.00 and 2.00.

Going back to the area under the curve:
For the Standard Normal Distribution the area under the curve is 1.
The area under the curve between -1.00 and 1.00 standard deviaitons from the mean of zero (Z-Scores) is 0.682.
The area under the curve within -2.00 and 2.00 Z-Scores is 0.954 and 0.997 for Z-Scores between -3.00 and 3.00.
The area under the curve below the mean is 0.50. The area under the curve above the mean is 0.50.
Again consider the Z-Score of 1.00. The area under the curve from Negative Infinity Z-Score to 1.00 Z-Score
is 0.841. The area above 1.00 Z-Score to Positive Infinity Z-Score is 1 - 0.841 = 0.159.

Another way to think about the area under the curve is in the form of probability of randomly finding a particlar Z-Score
above, below or between certain thresholds.
All possible Z-Scores for a Standard Normal Distribution are represented by the area under the curve, which is 1.
Half the area is below the mean and half the Z-Scores will fall below the mean. 34.1% of the area under the curve is
between the mean and 1.00 Z-Scores. Therefore, if you are randomly selecting items from your data set, 34.1% of them will have
a Z-Score greater than zero and less than 1.00. 15.9% of the items will have a Z-Score greater than 1.00 and 84.1% of the
Z-Scores will be less than 1.00.

The area under the curve up to a certain Z-Score is known as the Culative Distribution Function.
The CDF of 1.00 Z-Score is 0.841. The CDF of 0.00 Z-Score is 0.50. The CDF of -2.00 Z-Score is 0.021.
Therefore, the CDF is the probability of an item in the data set (distribution) having a Z-Score of less than
the chosen Z-Score. Again, the CDF of -2.00 Z-Score is 0.021. The probablility of an item in the data set
having a Z-Score of less than -2.00 Z-Scores is 2.1%. The probabitly of an item having a Z-Score or greater
than -2.00 is 97.9%.

It is important to note that these probabilities are for ranges of Z-Scores.
Remember, there are in infinite amount of Z-Scores. Therefore, the probabitily of one particular Z-Score is
1 divided by infinity which is essentially zero. Anything divided by infinity is basically zero.
Recall we could produce the Standard Normal Curve by feeding a Z-Scores (x-values) into the
equation for the curve to produce a y-value. This is a function. This particular function is referred to as a
coniuous function because it can take in and out put an infinte number of values. In contrast a discrete function
would take in a finite or infinite number of values and output a finite number of values.
Since the Standard Normal Curve is produced by a continous function, the area below the curve within specific ranges
is calculated with the maths of calculus through the method of integration. The examples used so far have been Z-Scores
that are marked on the graph. But what is we need the area between Negative Infinity Z-Score and 1.22 Z-Score?
This is the same as the CDF of 1.22 Z-Score. For this we would go to a CDF table which are pre-calculated areas under
the Standard Normal Curve for a large amount of Z-Scores. The area listed on the table is the area from Neg Inf Z-Score
to the chosen Z-Score, in other words the CDF of the chosen Z-Score. Of course the table does not have an infinite amount
of Z-Scores. Typically, the table lists CDFs for Z-Scores from -3.999 to 0.00 in an interval .001.
The area under the curve at -3.999 Z-Score is 0.00003. The Standard Normal Distribution has symetry.
As a result, the area above the curve at 3.999 Z-Score is 0.0003. Therefore, 99.99994% of the area under the
curve lies within 4.00 Z-Scores or 4.00 standard deviations from the mean.

Suppose you need the CDF for 0.73 Z-Score:
0.73 Z-Score CDF = 1 - (-0.73 Z-Score CDF)
The CDF for -0.73 Z-Score is 0.23270. Therefore the CDF for 0.73 Z-Score = 1 - 0.23270 = 0.76730.
The area under the Standard Normal Curve from Neg Inf Z-Score to 0.73 Z-Score is 0.76730.
The probability that an item from the data set has a Z-Score of less than 0.73 is 76.73%

Suppose you want the probablilty that a Z-Score is less than 0.73 and greater than 0.50.
The solution is: 0.73 Z-Score CDF - 0.50 Z-Score CDF = 0.76730 - 0.69146 = 0.07584.

S = Current Stock Price
K = Strike Price
t = time to expiration = (days to expitation / 365)
r = risk free rate for the time to expiration (treasury yield that corresponds to t)
var/2 = variance divided by 2
vol = sqrt(variance)
var = variance = vol * vol = vol^2 = vol**2

LN(K/S) is: S * e^? = K .....in other words LN(K/S) = ?.....LN(K/S) is the exponent to e that makes S*e^?=K
e = Euler's Number which is approximately 2.718281828
LN(K/S) is the % distance from K to S (continously compounded)

((r - var/2)*t) = mean (mean of the normal distribution)
in short: a risky asset's return has two components: risk free rate of return +/- the risky rate of return
all risky assets in a risk neutral world will increase by the risk free rate over the time period.
in addition, each risk assets will increase or decrease by their unique risky rate of return (which is assumed to by normally distributed)
r will be the mean return with one caveat:
risky asset's returns will be eroded by half the variance over the time period.
subtracting var/2 from the mean is an approximation of the Geometirc Mean (Geometirc Mean = Compunded Annual Growth Rate (CAGR))
Therefore r is Arithmetic Mean (average). Subtracting var/2*t from the Arithmetic Mean will give us an estimate of the CAGR.
This can also be translated into a Stock Price. The Stock Price that represents the mean of the distribution is :
SMean = S * e^[((r - var/2)*t)]


[ vol * sqrt(time)] = one standard deviaiton for the time period

d2 = { [ LN(K/S) - ((r - var/2)*t) ] / [ vol * sqrt(t)] } * -1
d1 = d2 + [ vol * sqrt(time)]


d2 is a Z-Score.
LN(K/S) is the continously compounded rate that makes the Stock = Strike.
In other words, the rate of return needed to make the Stike In-the-Money.
((r - var/2)*t) is the mean of the distribution.

LN(K/S) - ((r - var/2)*t) can be re-written as : LN(K/SMean)
So, LN(K/S) - ((r - var/2)*t) is just the continously compounded percentage difference between the Strike and the Mean of the Distribution.
This is all divided by One Standard Deviation: vol * sqrt(t)
This is a Z-Score. It tells us how may std devs the Strike is from the Mean.
A CDF Table will tells us the probability of a lower Z-Score than the one we calculated.
The Strike Px remains fixed. The Stock Px can varry. So it is the Stock Px that can change the Z-Score.
So, the Z-Score tells the probability of a Stock Px lower than the Strike Px at expiration.
However, we need the probabiltiy of the Stock being greater than the Strike at Expiration.
Or rather, the probability of the Call having a positive payout at Expiraton.
Therefore, the Z-Score must by multiplied by -1. Then we have d2.
N(d2) is simply the the probability associated with the d2 (Z-Score * -1) we calc'd on the CDF table.
Again, N(d2) is the probablilty the Strike is In-the-Money at Expiration. Not by how much, just simply if the Stock is greater than the Strike.
Another way to think about it is that if the Call is exercised on Expiration, then the owner will payout the Strike Price.
If this payout of the Strike Price happens, it will happen in the future, at Expiration.
Therefore N(d2) is the proability of payout the Present Value of the Strike Price (PVofK).
In the Call Value below, this is represented by the term : [ PVofK * N(d2) ]
[ PVofK * N(d2) ] = Expected Value of Paying Out the Strike Px at Expiration


We know that d2 is a Z-Score.
d1 = d2 + [ vol * sqrt(time)] , which is d2 plus one std dev.
So, d1 is just another Z-Score. You can see in the model d1 is used in the term: [ S * N(d1) ]
Most books do not explain d1 very well. It us usually called the Expected Value of Paying out the Contignet Receipt of the Stock.
Or, if the Strike is In-The-Money it is the Call Payout (the extra value above the strike you get if the Stock is greater than the Strike)

Some greater insight on d1 can be gained by examining what Stock Px do we need in the d2 equation to make d2 = d1 ?
Lets call this the StockUp Price since to get d1 we are always adding One Std Dev to d2.
Therefore StockUpPx will always be greater than StockPx.
StockUpPx = StockPx * e^(var * t) ......so, another way write d1 is :

d1 = { [ LN(K / StockPxUp) - ((r - var/2)*t) ] / [ vol * sqrt(t)] } * -1

Another question to ask is: If N(d2) is the probability of a positive call payout, then
lets consider only the Stock Prices that cause a positive call payout. Essentially only the Stock Prices
that are greater than Strike at Expiration. Lets call those PosStockPx.
What is the Expected Value of PosStockPx ?
Expected Value of PosStockPx = S * N(d1) / N(d2)
therefore, Positive Call Payout = PosStockPx - Strike
Call Value = Expected Value of a Positive Call Payout = N(d2) * Positive Call Payout
This can also be shown with Monte Carlo simulations to value the call option.
We can isolate the simulations that result in a stock price at expiration that is greater than strke.
The ratio of: Simulations Greater Than Strike / Total Simulations = N(d2) approximation
Then we can calc the Average of the Simulations Greater Than Strike.
PV of Average of the Simulations Greater Than Strike - Strike = Avg Call Payout if Strike is In-the-Money at Expiration
Avg Call Payout if Strike is In-the-Money at Expiration = Positive Call Payout in BS Model
Expected Call Payout if Strike is In-The-Money at Expir = Avg Call Payout if Strike is In-the-Money at Expiration * N(d2) approximation
Call Value = Expected Call Payout if Strike is In-The-Money at Expir


C = Call Value = [ S * N(d1) ] - [ PVofK * N(d2) ]
P = Put Value = PVofK - S + C

or

P = Put Value = [ -S * N(-d1) ] + [ PVofK * N( (vol*sqrt(t)) - d1) ]
C = Call Value = S - PVofK + P
